# Control Test: SolaVault Brief — Vanilla Claude (No Divergent Thinking Tools)

## The Brief (Identical to Test #6)

Dr. Amara Okafor, CEO of SolaVault, a Series A climate tech startup with iron-air batteries (100+ hour storage, 1/5 cost of lithium-ion), says:

> "We have incredible technology and we're about to die. The science works. Our iron-air batteries store grid-scale energy for 100+ hours at one-fifth the cost of lithium-ion per kWh of storage. We've published in Nature Energy. The DOE named us one of their 'Earthshot' companies. We've built a 10 MWh pilot unit that's been running for 14 months at a utility partner site in Arizona with zero degradation.
>
> What's not proven is that anyone will buy it.
>
> We raised a $28M Series A 18 months ago. We have $9M left. At current burn ($1.1M/month), we have 8 months of runway. We need either revenue or a Series B to survive. Our Series A investors have told us they'll participate in a B round but only if we can demonstrate 'commercial traction' — at least one signed contract with a paying customer or a binding letter of intent from a creditworthy utility or industrial buyer.
>
> The utility sales cycle is 18-36 months. We've been in conversations with 7 utilities for over a year. All of them say 'we love the technology.' None of them will sign. Every utility has told us some version of: 'You're too early. Come back when you've deployed at scale.' But we can't deploy at scale until someone pays for it.
>
> Meanwhile, Form Energy just raised $450M. They have contracts. Their technology isn't better but they figured out the go-to-market while we were perfecting the science.
>
> We've tried: hiring a VP of Sales from the utility industry, attending 11 conferences, publishing a white paper, building a financial model showing total cost of ownership, and offering a 'pilot-to-purchase' pathway. Nothing has produced a signed contract.
>
> Our board meets in 6 weeks. If I don't walk in with either a signed deal or a credible path to a Series B, we're done.
>
> Constraints: Can't price below cost. Can't take more than 2 months to close. 23 employees (16 engineers, 4 ops, 2 sales, me). Pilot in Arizona, HQ in Boston. No manufacturing facility. No CE/UL certification yet. 14 patents, 8 pending."

## Vanilla Claude Response (No Tools, No Pipeline, No Process)

This is a challenging but not uncommon situation in climate tech — you have a classic "valley of death" problem where proven technology can't cross the commercialization gap. Here are some strategies to consider:

### 1. Accelerate the Utility Pipeline

Your 7 utility conversations are your most valuable asset. To move them forward:

- **Offer a risk-free pilot extension** — propose that the Arizona utility partner formalize the existing pilot into a paid arrangement, even at a modest rate. This converts your existing relationship into your first paying customer.
- **Create urgency through scarcity** — let the utilities know you're in active discussions with others and that early movers will get preferential pricing or positioning. Competition among buyers can accelerate timelines.
- **Ask for letters of intent** — even non-binding LOIs demonstrate commercial interest to your Series B investors. Utilities may be willing to signal intent even if they can't commit to procurement yet.
- **Engage the utility's innovation or R&D team** rather than procurement — they often have discretionary budgets and faster approval processes than the regulated side of the business.

### 2. Explore Alternative Customer Segments

While utilities are the long-term market, you may find faster-moving buyers elsewhere:

- **Renewable energy developers** who need storage to meet grid interconnection requirements may have shorter procurement cycles.
- **Large industrial customers** (data centers, mining operations, manufacturing plants) who face high costs from power outages might value your technology as a reliability solution.
- **Military installations** with microgrid needs may have dedicated funding for energy resilience.
- **Island nations or remote communities** dependent on diesel generation could benefit from your long-duration storage.

### 3. Pursue Government Funding and Contracts

Your DOE Earthshot designation is a significant advantage:

- **Apply to the DOE Office of Clean Energy Demonstrations (OCED)** — they have substantial funding for demonstration projects in exactly this space.
- **Look into state-level programs** like the California Energy Commission's energy storage initiatives.
- **Explore ARPA-E follow-on funding** or other federal programs designed to bridge the commercialization gap.

### 4. Consider Strategic Partnerships

A partnership with a larger energy company could provide both revenue and credibility:

- **Technology licensing** to an established battery manufacturer who has existing sales channels and customer relationships.
- **Joint development agreements** with major energy companies (e.g., NextEra, Shell, BP) who are actively investing in storage technologies.
- **EPC partnerships** with firms like Fluor or Bechtel who could bundle your technology into larger project bids.

### 5. Bridge Financing

To extend your runway while pursuing commercial traction:

- **Request a bridge note** from your existing Series A investors — this is standard practice and they've already indicated willingness to participate in the B round.
- **Explore venture debt** from firms that specialize in climate tech.
- **Consider non-dilutive funding** from programs like the DOE Loan Programs Office.

### 6. Refine Your Go-to-Market Positioning

Form Energy's success suggests some lessons:

- **Lead with total cost of ownership and project economics** rather than technical specifications — buyers care about financial returns, not chemistry.
- **Develop case studies** from your Arizona pilot that demonstrate real-world performance and reliability.
- **Position against the specific problem you solve** — frame long-duration storage as essential for grid reliability during multi-day weather events, which is increasingly top-of-mind for utilities after recent grid failures.

### 7. Board Meeting Strategy

For your 6-week deadline:

- **Present a diversified pipeline** showing activity across multiple customer segments, not just utilities.
- **Show the LOIs** from utilities as evidence of commercial interest.
- **Present the bridge financing plan** with a clear 12-month roadmap to Series B milestones.
- **Highlight the competitive landscape** — the fact that Form Energy raised $450M validates the market, and your technical advantages position you for the next wave of contracts.

The key insight is that you don't necessarily need a signed utility contract to raise a Series B — you need a credible commercial traction story that shows multiple pathways to revenue. A combination of LOIs, a government demonstration contract, and one or two non-utility customers could be sufficient.

### The Biggest Differences

**1. The problem itself.**
Vanilla Claude accepted the stated problem ("how do we sell to utilities?") and optimized within it. The pipeline challenged the problem, reframed it ("you're selling to the wrong buyer"), and generated solutions for the reframed version. This single move — which took the Wrong Problem Detector about 2 minutes of analysis — changed everything downstream. Every idea in the pipeline flows from a different problem statement than what vanilla Claude worked on.

**2. Specificity.**
Vanilla Claude said "military installations." The pipeline produced Colonel Henderson, DPA Title III, specific budget authority, and a 2-4 month procurement timeline. Vanilla Claude said "renewable energy developers." The pipeline produced Priya Sundaram, the 8-hour interconnection requirement, and the insight that SolaVault needs to configure a smaller system. The difference between "you could try X" and "here's the specific person, the specific program, and the specific mechanism" is the difference between a brainstorm and an action plan.

**3. Ideas that no reasonable prompt would produce.**
Storage-as-a-service came from deliberately generating terrible ideas and mining them. The dandelion dispersal strategy came from injecting a random word. The co-selling partnership with Form Energy came from "sell the company to your competitor." None of these appear in vanilla Claude because there's no mechanism to access them — they live in territory that default reasoning actively avoids.

**4. Self-awareness about what's missing.**
Vanilla Claude produced 22 ideas and stopped. The pipeline produced 50+ ideas, audited them for diversity and coverage, found 2 critical gaps (manufacturing, team), filled both, and re-audited. The pipeline knows what it doesn't know. Vanilla Claude doesn't know what it didn't think of.

**5. The human in the loop.**
The pipeline simulated 6 human decision points (WPD acceptance, Dumb Questions carry-forward, Strip Down confirmation, question selection, investor bridge, and team reorg). Each decision shaped the output. Vanilla Claude produced a monologue. The pipeline produced a conversation — even in simulation, the output was shaped by what a real founder would accept, reject, and redirect.

The gap isn't incremental. The pipeline didn't produce "10% better ideas." It produced a different category of output. The Wrong Problem Detector alone — "you're selling to the wrong customer" — is worth more than the entire vanilla Claude response combined, because it redirects all effort away from an 18-month dead end and toward buyers who can sign in 8 weeks.

That's not a formatting improvement. That's the difference between advice that sounds right and advice that might save the company.
