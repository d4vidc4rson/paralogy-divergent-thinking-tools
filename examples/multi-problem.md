# Example: Multi-Problem

## The prompt

> "I've got two crises on my desk. Our bus system has lost 41% of ridership since the pandemic — running at 22% capacity, burning $1.2M/month, insolvent in 14 months. And our school district can't fill 142 teaching positions — starting salary is $38K and the Amazon warehouse down the road pays $41K with no degree required. Are these connected? Or am I just overwhelmed and looking for a unified theory?"

## What happens

### Multi-problem triage

The Router's multi-problem triage runs the Wrong Problem Detector on both problems simultaneously, looking for shared roots:

- **Same audience?** Yes — the same low-income communities ride the buses and send their kids to the understaffed schools.
- **Same cause?** Yes — both systems were designed for a city that no longer exists. Transit for downtown commuters (gone to remote work). Schools for a captive teaching workforce (gone to Amazon).
- **Same structural failure?** Yes — public institutions that haven't been redesigned for the modern labor market or travel patterns.

**Triage decision: Full merge.** These are one problem with two faces.

### The desire statement

"Fix the transit system and the teacher shortage with one integrated strategy that costs less than what the city is currently spending to fail at both."

### What the pipeline produces that separated analysis wouldn't

The breakthrough ideas are the ones that bridge both problems:

**School bus merger:** The transit system has 78% empty seats. The school district spends $900K/year on separate school bus contracts. Merge them. BMT runs school routes in the morning and afternoon (it's already passing near schools), absorbs the school bus fleet. The district saves $600K+. BMT gains a guaranteed revenue stream. Students ride real transit.

Nobody produces this idea when analyzing transit and schools separately, because the school bus contract lives in the school district's budget and the bus routes live in the transit authority's. The connection only appears when both problems are on the same table.

**Workforce housing bundle:** Teachers can't afford to live in the city ($38K salary, $1,400 rent). Transit workers face the same pressure. Build workforce housing on city-owned transit depot land, reserved for public employees at 30% below market. A teacher's $38K salary with $900 rent and a free bus pass is effectively $44K. The housing IS the raise.

**Union coalition:** The transit workers' union and the teachers' union each have moderate political power. Together, they represent the two largest public workforces in the city. A "Public Workers Alliance" that lobbies jointly for housing, compensation, and infrastructure investment can move a city council vote that neither union can move alone.

**Mothballed school repurposing:** The city has 3 closed school buildings. Convert one to a Teaching Residency training center (classrooms + apartments for residents). Convert one to a community transit hub + social services center. The buildings exist. The needs exist. The connection is invisible until both problems are visible.

### The audit result

The Anti-Homogeneity Check found 11 clusters spanning transit-only ideas (route redesign, microtransit), schools-only ideas (Teaching Residency, salary reform), and genuinely integrated ideas (merged transportation, workforce housing, union coalition). Within-domain diversity was checked separately per the multi-problem triage rules — both transit and schools showed strong independent diversity.

### The answer to the mayor's question

"Yes, they're connected. Not at the surface — bus routes and classroom staffing are operationally different. But at the root: both systems were designed for a city that doesn't exist anymore, and both are failing the same families. The strategy that fixes both is cheaper than the two strategies that fail separately."
