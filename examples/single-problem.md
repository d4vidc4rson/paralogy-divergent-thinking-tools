# Example: Single Problem

## The prompt

> "I'm the founder of a climate tech startup. We've built iron-air batteries that store grid-scale energy for 100+ hours at one-fifth the cost of lithium-ion. The science is proven — published in Nature Energy, DOE Earthshot designation, 14-month pilot running with zero degradation. But we can't close a single sale. Utilities love us but won't sign — their procurement cycle is 18-36 months and we have 8 months of runway. Form Energy raised $450M with arguably worse technology because they figured out go-to-market while we were perfecting the science. Our board meets in 6 weeks. If I don't have a signed deal or a credible path to Series B, we're done."

## What vanilla Claude produces

A well-organized 7-section consulting memo: accelerate the utility pipeline, explore alternative customers (developers, industrial, military), pursue government funding, consider strategic partnerships, seek bridge financing, refine positioning, and prepare for the board meeting. ~22 ideas. Competent. Safe. Most of it the founder has already heard.

## What the pipeline produces

**The Wrong Problem Detector** reframes the entire problem in the first 2 minutes: "You don't have a sales problem. You have a customer-segment problem. You're selling to the slowest buyer in the economy while running out of money."

That single move changes everything downstream. Instead of "how to sell to utilities faster" (which every other advisor would work on), the pipeline generates against "who needs this technology and can sign a contract in 8 weeks?"

**The Persona Divergence Engine** produces Colonel Henderson — a military base commander with DOD Defense Production Act budget authority for exactly this kind of energy technology, a 2-4 month procurement timeline, and nobody from the startup has ever called him. It produces Elena Vasquez — a rural community organizer with an approved FEMA resilience grant for battery storage and no vendor who can provide 100-hour capability. It produces Priya Sundaram — a solar developer who needs 8-hour storage for interconnection bids and can sign in 6 weeks.

These aren't categories on a whiteboard. They're people with money and urgency who the startup didn't know existed.

**Bad on Purpose** generates "give the technology away for free" and mines it to find the storage-as-a-service model — deploy first, charge for energy dispatched, zero upfront risk to the customer. This came from deliberately bad thinking, not from conventional strategy.

**Random Injection** introduces "dandelion" and produces the dispersal strategy: stop nurturing one utility relationship for 18 months. Send a 2-page spec sheet to 200 buyers across 5 segments in one week. Most won't respond. A few will land in disturbed ground (the mining company that just had a $2M outage, the FEMA community that just survived a 72-hour blackout). SolaVault doesn't need 200 customers. It needs 3.

**The Anti-Homogeneity Check** grades the combined output A- (7 clusters, good diversity) but catches two gaps: no manufacturing/delivery plan, and no team reorganization plan. **The Gap-Fill Loop** fills both: contract manufacturing through existing partners, and a reallocation of the 23-person team where the CTO leads technical sales conversations (engineers sell to engineers) while the VP Sales manages the utility LOI campaign.

**Final output: 65+ ideas, 9 clusters, Grade A.** The problem was reframed. The buyer segments are specific. The funding programs are named. The organizational plan exists. The control comparison shows 15 ideas the pipeline produced that vanilla Claude didn't touch.

## The difference

Vanilla Claude answered the question the founder asked: "how do I sell to utilities?"

The pipeline answered the question the founder needed: "who can you sell to before you die?"
