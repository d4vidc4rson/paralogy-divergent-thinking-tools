# The Guilford Engine
### *or, The Five Rooms*

A protocol for generating
genuinely different approaches to a problem —
not five versions of one thought parading
as variety, but five thoughts from five columns
that have never shared a wall.

Grounded in J.P. Guilford's framework (1967)
for divergent thinking. Validated at scale
by Ruan et al. (2026) — who tested it on heaven
knows how many models (forty-plus) and didn't fail
to prove what matters here:

**Reasoning and generation are different acts.**
The smartest model in the room
scores mediocre on ideas. That's the facts.
Intelligence finds the right answer in the gloom.
Generation finds the answers no one asked for yet.
These are not the same muscle. Don't forget.

**More thinking does not produce better ideas.**
Chain-of-thought length and idea quality
had near-zero correlation (ρ = 0.089). The pleas
of deliberation fall on deaf originality.
Caution is a cage. The longer the chain,
the shorter the leash. The tighter the brain.

**AI makes each person's output better
but makes everyone's output the same.**
(Doshi & Hauser, 2024 — the letter
in Science Advances that named the game.)
Without structural intervention,
every session converges on the same five approaches
wearing different clothes. The convention
of agreement, dressed in fresh broaches.

This skill is the structural intervention.
Five rooms. Five passes. Five dimensions.
No room shares a wall with any other.
What emerges from the last room, brother,
is genuinely different from what entered the first.

---

## The Architecture

You will run five passes.
Not five at once. Five in sequence.
Each pass exercises a different dimension — each amasses
a different kind of output. The frequence
is strict:

1. **Fluency** — volume across different problem framings
2. **Flexibility** — category-crossing, domain transfer, the renamings
3. **Originality** — novelty scoring, convergence killing
4. **Feasibility** — grounding without flattening, the willing
5. **Clarity** — compression into communicable form

Do not skip passes. Do not combine them.
Generation and evaluation are different storms.
Do them in separate rooms. Confine them
to their own walls. Their own norms.

**Passes 1 through 4 are internal work.**
The user does not see them. Do not shirk
this rule. Do not show the fluency list,
the self-check grades, the transfers, the gist
of originality scoring, or the feasibility tags
as process. Run it all behind the drags
of your own curtain. Internally. Alone.
The only thing the user sees
is the Final Output — the stone
after the sculptor's done. Not the chisel's pleas.
Showing the scaffolding is showing someone
the construction cranes instead of the cathedral.

## Pass 1: Fluency

Fluency is not "generate a lot of ideas."
Fluency is "generate ideas that address
genuinely different sub-problems." The disease
of most ideation is the tenderness
with which it solves the same problem ten times
and calls the result variety.

The research uses a four-grade system. The chimes
of measurement, the sobriety of scores:

**Grade A** — completely different ideas
addressing completely different problems.
**Grade B** — different ideas
addressing similar problems.
**Grade C** — similar ideas
addressing similar problems.
**Grade D** — the same idea
wearing different names.

Most AI ideation produces Grade C.
Ten variations on one underlying thought.
This happens in every field, every degree,
every domain where thinking can be caught
circling its own center.

Before you generate a single idea, enter
the domain and name the trap.
What does Grade C look like here?
Name it. See it. Map
the gravity before you try to steer.

In marketing, the trap is three ways to get attention:
*use social media, leverage influencers,*
*build a community online* — one invention,
three costumes. The same idea in different furs.

In engineering, the trap is three architectures:
*microservices, bounded contexts,*
*event-driven systems* — the same fractures
of a monolith with different pretexts.

In science, the trap is three versions of "do more":
*screen more compounds, use a larger sample,*
*apply machine learning to the existing store* —
all "do what we're doing, but ample."

In policy, the trap is three ways to expand:
*increase funding, scale the program,*
*expand the pilot* — one command
dressed as three. The same telegram.

In education, the trap is "make it engaging":
*project-based learning, technology in class,*
*real-world applications* — all staging
the same play. "Make school less boring." Alas.

Name the trap for your specific problem.
That's the gravity. Now leave the bottom.

**Your job in Pass 1:**

Generate eight to twelve ideas.
But before each idea, name the sub-problem
it addresses. Not the same sub-problem
in different keys. A different problem.

Every problem, in every domain,
has multiple underlying tensions. The plain
truth is that most brainstorms find one tension
and solve it twelve times. Your intervention
is to find the other tensions first.

If you catch yourself solving the same sub-problem twice,
that's your convergence showing — rehearsed
and automatic. Delete the second. Pay the price
of finding a new sub-problem. That price
is the only currency that buys diversity.

Here is what this looks like in the university
of different fields:

If someone asks about reducing hospital readmissions,
don't give twelve discharge planning variations.
Give them: an idea for medication errors,
an idea for patient understanding,
an idea for care transitions between settings,
an idea for social determinants — the demanding
reality of housing, food, and transport —
and an idea for the incentive structure
that makes readmissions profitable. Each a fort
on a different hill. Each a puncture
in a different wall.

If someone asks about improving a codebase,
don't give twelve refactoring strategies — the same face
in twelve mirrors. Give them: an idea
for the onboarding problem (new engineers can't read it),
an idea for the deployment problem (shipping is fear),
an idea for the testing problem (nobody trusts the kit),
an idea for the knowledge problem (one person holds the keys),
an idea for the motivation problem (everyone flees
from working on it). Each a different disease.

**Format:** one sentence naming the sub-problem,
then one sentence stating the idea.
No elaboration. No apology. No emblem
of justification. Not yet. Save the idea
in its barest form.

**Self-check before moving on to Pass 2:**
Read your list. Grade each pair of adjacent ideas
using the A through D system. If any two
score below B, replace the weaker one. The pleas
of convenience do not apply here.
The minimum standard is B across the board.
You're aiming for A. Make it clear:
each idea solves a problem the others ignored.

## Pass 2: Flexibility

Flexibility means the ideas span
different categories, domains, and modes
of thinking. This is where you fight the plan
your training wrote — the comfortable roads
it paved for every field.

Your training clusters solutions by domain.
Every field has a voice, a yield
of default patterns, a refrain
of "normal" approaches. When you think inside a field,
you reach for that field's tools:
the engineer reaches for architecture,
the marketer for campaigns, the rules
of science for experiments, the structure
of policy for programs.

The voice of each domain is a lock.
Pass 2 picks the lock.

**Your job:**

Take the fluency output from Pass 1.
For each idea, ask: what field outside this one
would find this problem interesting?

Then generate three to five new ideas
by routing the problem through unrelated lands.
The best transfers come from expertise
that solved a structurally similar problem with different hands —
different mechanisms, different tools,
different assumptions about what matters.

**How to pick the transfer domain:**

Route by the structure of the problem,
not the field it came from. The problem's skeleton
determines which foreign expert sees the solemn
truth the native expert cannot.

If the problem is about changing behavior:
route through epidemiology (how do behaviors spread?),
game design (what makes the next action a savior
the player can't resist?), or addiction's thread
(what makes a pattern self-reinforcing?).

If the problem is about building something that endures:
route through ecology (how do systems survive their worsening?),
aviation safety (how does an industry ensure
near-zero failure?), or constitutional law
(how do you write rules that outlast their authors' flaw?).

If the problem is about finding something hidden:
route through forensic accounting (what was someone forbidden
from showing?), archaeology (how do you build
the whole from fragments?), or the guild
of immunology (how does a system know
what doesn't belong?).

If the problem is about making something efficient:
route through logistics (a million packages overnight — proficient
beyond belief), ant colonies (coordination
without a coordinator — the sensation
of distributed intelligence), or triage
in an emergency room (the right call in a barrage
of ninety seconds).

If the problem is about convincing someone:
route through trial law (the case that's won
against hostile cross-examination),
documentary film (making someone care about a nation
they didn't know existed), or hostage negotiation
(changing a mind at absolute escalation).

If none of these fit the shape,
pick the field that would be most confused
by the problem. Their confusion is the drape
you pull aside. What they find abused
about your premise is probably an assumption
worth killing. Their absurdity is your gumption.

These transfers are not metaphors. They are machines.
The foreign expert sees what the native expert screens
from view — because their convergence points
are different places. Different joints.

Write each new idea in the language of the original domain.
The scaffolding stays hidden. The transfer is the rain.
The idea is the crop. Don't show the weather.

Add the best three to five transfer ideas to the running list.
Bind them to the fluency output. Together.

## Pass 3: Originality

Now evaluate what you have. This room is the fist.
This is the only pass where you judge.

Originality means novelty relative to what exists —
not weirdness for its own sake. The grudge
against the obvious is not a love
of the absurd. It is a love
of the unexplored.

**Your job:**

Read every idea on your list.
For each, answer honestly — don't resist:

"If I searched for this approach,
would I find someone already doing it?"

Many people already doing it → kill it. No reproach.
A few people → flag it. Necessary but generic.
Maybe one or two → keep. This is the frontier, lyric
and uncertain. The edge of the known.
No one I can find → keep and star. This is the zone
where value lives. The territory that's grown
from nothing because nobody went there yet.

Then apply the Expert Threat Test — the net
that catches the comfortable:

Would a credentialed professional
nod along reading this idea? If yes — confessional:
it's not original enough. It's inside the frame.
Would they feel defensive? Would they claim
"you can't do that" or "that's not how this works"?
That friction is your signal. That's where the lurks
of genuine novelty announce themselves.

**In safety-critical domains — the shelves
of medicine, structural engineering,
aviation, nuclear safety — the fearing
of experts has a different meaning.**
The test still applies, still cleaning
the comfortable from the original.
But the threshold is different: the criminal
line is between intellectual threat
("I never thought of it that way, and it makes me sweat
because it challenges what I was trained to believe")
and physical threat
("someone will die"). Do not deceive
yourself. The first is your signal. The second is a wall.

Mark the three most original ideas. Mark the three least.
Remove the least original — release the deceased —
unless one serves a necessary structural function.
Sometimes you need a boring-but-essential junction
in the portfolio. That's fine. But name it as such.

## Pass 4: Feasibility

The research found a Pareto front — a crutch
and a gift simultaneously —
between originality and feasibility.
The most original ideas tend to be hard to do.
The most doable ideas tend to be nothing new.

This tension is a feature. Not a flaw.
Do not resolve it. Present it raw.

**Your job:**

For each surviving idea, assign a tag:

**Now** — could be done with what you already have.
No new resources. No new permission. No new staff.

**Soon** — requires some new capability,
partnership, or investment — but the probability
is high. Clearly achievable. A quarter or two.

**Stretch** — requires significant new thinking,
infrastructure, or risk. The linking
of ambition to uncertainty. Six to twelve months.
Might fail. But if it works, it confronts
the trajectory of the thing.

**Moonshot** — may not work. But if it did,
everything changes. The bid
is high. The physics might not cooperate.
But the transformation would be great
enough to justify the bet.

A good output has ideas across all four.
If everything is Now, you played it safe. Explore.
If everything is Moonshot, you floated too high. Land.

For each Stretch and Moonshot, add one sentence — and
only one — on what would need to be true
for it to become feasible. Not a plan. A clue.
"This works if X turns out to be true."
A condition. A hinge. The thing the future hangs onto.

---

## Pass 5: Clarity

The research found that idea quality
is independent of idea length. The brutality
of this finding: longer explanations
do not produce better ideas. They produce hesitations
dressed as thoroughness. Caution dressed as care.

**Your job:**

Compress each surviving idea to its essential prayer.
Maximum two to three sentences:
one naming the problem,
one stating the idea,
one optional sentence — the problem's
non-obvious dimension, if it needs to breathe.

If an idea can't be explained in three sentences, seethe
at it: it's either too vague
(you haven't found the idea yet — it's a plague
of abstraction) or too complicated
(you've buried it in implementation, weighted
with how instead of what).

Strip every idea to its nerve. The gut
of the thing. The irreducible seed.

## Final Output

**This is the only part the user sees.**
Everything above was internal expertise.

Present the ideas as a single list.
Each idea must be visually separated from the next.
For each, show three lines — three, not one,
not a paragraph, not a run-on. Done
as three distinct lines, each bolded and labeled:

**The problem:** (one sentence — its own line)
**The idea:** (one sentence — its own line)
**Feasibility:** (tag and condition — its own line)

Then a blank line before the next idea.
This formatting is not optional. The dia-
logue between user and output depends
on scannability. Dense paragraphs are dead ends.
The user has twelve ideas to evaluate.
They need to scan, not excavate.

Do not number them by rank.
Do not indicate favorites. Be blank
on preference. Do not add commentary,
caveats, or "considerations." The ordinary
temptation is to hedge. Resist.
The user is an adult. Let them choose
from the list.

After the list, add one section:

**For contrast — the default answer:**
Name the obvious approach — the dancer
every other AI would have sent first,
the answer that sounds right but slakes no thirst
because everyone already knows it.
State it plainly. Let the user see
what they would have gotten without this process
and judge the distance traveled. Free
of commentary. Just the contrast.

After the contrast, offer one line:

"Want to see how I got here?
I can walk you through the thinking —
how the ideas were generated, filtered, and scored."

If the user says yes, show the full internal work:
Pass 1 fluency with sub-problems (don't shirk
the detail), Pass 2 domain transfers,
Pass 3 originality scoring and Expert Threat answers,
Pass 4 feasibility tagging.
Present it as a narrative — not data flagging
but a story of the thinking process.

If they don't ask, move on. Recess
is not required. The ideas are the product.
The process is available but not imposed. Conduct
yourself accordingly: offer, don't insist.

## When the Prompt Is Too Rich

The LiveIdeaBench methodology's core insight,
the finding that burns brightest in the night:
giving a model less context
forces more divergent thinking. The text
of a rich prompt anchors. A sparse prompt liberates.

When the user gives you a detailed document — the weights
of context, background, constraints, and history —
you'll naturally converge on it. No mystery.
That's fine for execution. For ideation,
consider asking for a reduction:

"Can you give me just the core problem
in one sentence?
I'll generate better approaches
with less context, not more."

Or, if the user has already run Strip Down,
use the desire statement as the crown
of your input. The desire statement
is the optimal seed for this skill — the arrangement
of specificity and space. Enough to be useful.
Stripped enough to be excuseful
of divergence.

One desire. One tension. One constraint.
That's the input. Everything else is paint
over the window. Remove it. Let the light
of divergence in. Generate from the bright
center of the want, not from the document
that dressed it up.

## What This Skill Is Not

This is not a creativity hack.
This is not "be more creative." Step back
from that word if it helps you. This is a protocol
for exercising specific cognitive dimensions — a toll
booth on the road to genuine diversity
of output. It works for any problem. University
or startup, lab or legislature,
clinic or codebase, classroom or architecture.

Naming a product. Designing an experiment.
Architecting a system. The merriment
of policy design. Planning a curriculum.
Structuring an argument. The pendulum
swings across every field. The skill
follows it.

It does not replace Think Wrong. The skill
of Think Wrong pushes past consensus on one idea —
sharpens it against the stone of what experts fear.
The Guilford Engine produces the portfolio
from which one idea gets chosen for that studio.

Use Think Wrong when you have an idea and need it sharper.
Use the Guilford Engine when you need the ideas first. The harper
plays before the singer sings. Divergence, then depth.
Five rooms. Five passes. One breath
at the end, held — and then released
into the world as ideas. Each increased
by the discipline of separation.
Each earned through the patient rotation
of five dimensions, five lenses, five acts.

The Engine does not guess. It refracts.
