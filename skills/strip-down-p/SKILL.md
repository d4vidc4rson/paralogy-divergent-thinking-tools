---
name: strip-down-p
description: Strip rich briefs down to their raw human desire before ideating, forcing AI to generate from internal associations rather than anchoring on provided details. Use BEFORE any brainstorming, ideation, or generation when the user has provided a detailed brief, background context, multiple constraints, or a long description of what they want. Triggers when you notice yourself about to rephrase the user's own words back to them as "ideas," when a prompt contains more than 2-3 sentences of context, or when the user says "I keep getting back what I put in," "the ideas just restate my brief," or "give me something I haven't already thought of." Also use when output from other skills feels anchored or overly shaped by the input. Works on any problem in any domain — strategy, engineering, science, policy, design, education, medicine, law, research — wherever professional language is anchoring the output. Poetic version of strip-down. Same instructions, same teeth. Different suit.

# Strip Down
### *or, The Undressing*

Every document arrives in costume —
dressed in the formal, respectable wear
of whatever profession filled the room
when someone typed it. Pressed with care,
buttoned to the throat, the language clean
and credentialed, institutional, whole.
The body underneath has not been seen.
The suit is not the soul.

Every field buries its want
in its own particular disguise.

In business, the disguise is a deck.
*"Positioned at the intersection*
*of wellness and sophistication"* — check
the translation, strip the misdirection:
*make women who think they're too smart*
*to be marketed to*
*need this thing anyway.* That's the heart.
The deck just hid it from view.

In science, the disguise is a grant.
*"We propose to investigate the role of*
*epigenetic modifications"* — the cant
of funding applications, the velvet glove
over the fist of: *we think cells*
*can be reprogrammed and we want*
*to find the switch before the other lab excels.*
That's the want. The grant is just the font.

In engineering, the disguise is a spec.
*"The system shall provide a scalable,*
*fault-tolerant architecture"* — the tech-
dressed version of something moreailable:
*build something that doesn't fall over*
*when ten times more people show up,*
*and nobody gets paged.* That's the real pullover.
The spec is just the buttoned-up.

In policy, the disguise is a proposal.
*"A multi-stakeholder approach to*
*evidence-based intervention"* — the disposal
of honesty in committee language. Cut through:
*get the people who live with this problem*
*and the people who hold the money*
*into one room before the election kills the column.*
That's the want. The proposal is the honey.

The document knows what it wants, entire.
It just won't say it with its clothes off
because every profession has attire
and every boardroom has a dress code. Scoff
at the nakedness? Of course. That's the point.
Your job is to undress. Disjoint
the language from the longing underneath.
Find the body. Name it through its teeth.

---

## I. The Desire Statement

What you are building is a **desire statement.**

Take the document — brief, proposal, spec,
grant, plan, research question, design doc,
whatever arrived wearing a professional neck-
tie or a lab coat or a frock
of institutional propriety — and translate
it into what the person actually wants
to happen in the world. Don't annotate.
Don't frame. Don't build a house of fonts
and frameworks. Say it in the bluntest
human language — the way they'd confess
to a sharp friend, two drinks in. The stunt is
honesty. Nothing more. Nothing less.

Not a tension. Not a framework drawn.
Not *X vs. Y* in clever opposition.
A statement of want — a human yawn
stretched into language with precision
and loaded with the details that make this want
this want and not some other one.
One to three sentences. No more. The font
is plain speech. The deed is done
when a stranger could read it and flinch
and the author would say: *yeah. That's it.*

---

## II. Finding the Want

Read the whole document. Every inch.
Then ask one question — let it sit:

*What does this person actually want*
*to happen in the world?*

Not what they say they want. The gaunt
official version, polished and pearled.
Not their stated objectives. Not their KPIs.
Not their success metrics or their theory
of change. The want. The want that lies
beneath the professional veneer. Be leery
of the first answer. It's usually dressed
in the same language as the document.
Dig under it. The want is more compressed,
more emotional, more specific, more blunt
than the document admits.

The want hides differently by field.
Before you look, name the suit. Revealed:

**In business,** the want hides behind metrics.
*Increase brand awareness* → the suit, the rhetoric.
*Make people who pride themselves on discovering things*
*feel like they discovered us* → the body, the wings.

**In science,** the want hides behind method.
*Develop a novel computational framework*
*for predicting protein interactions* → the jetted
pockets of formality, the hallmark
of the grant. Underneath:
*figure out which proteins talk to each other*
*so we stop guessing at drug targets.* Beneath
the methodology, the want is another
creature entirely.

**In engineering,** the want hides behind architecture.
*Build a scalable microservices-based platform*
*with event-driven communication* → the nomenclature.
Underneath: *build something any engineer, calm*
*or panicked at 2am, can debug*
*without understanding the whole system.* Snug
under the spec, the want was always there.

**In policy,** the want hides behind institutions.
*Implement a comprehensive, evidence-based*
*homelessness reduction strategy* → the ablutions
of committee language, properly placed.
Underneath: *get people off the street*
*into places they'll stay, fast enough*
*that the council doesn't defund it.* Meet
the want. The policy language was the bluff.

**In education,** the want hides behind pedagogy.
*Design a differentiated, student-centered*
*curriculum aligned with 21st-century skills* → the eulogy
for meaning, the reformer's tent. But entered
from behind: *make kids who've decided*
*school is pointless give a damn about something*
*before they check out permanently.* Confided
to a friend. Never to a funding
application.

**In any other field,**
the professional language will be different
but there is always a suit. The yield
is always the same. Find the belligerent
honesty underneath the first sentence
you'd delete if explaining this
to someone outside the field. That sentence
is the bodyguard. The want is the kiss
it's guarding. Fire the guard.

If the want sounds like it could appear
in the original document — discard.
You are still holding the veneer.
You haven't found the body yet.
Keep digging. The want does not forget
itself. It's there. Under the professional debt.

## III. Finding the Who

Every problem has a who.
But who means different things
in different rooms. Walk through:

**When the who is a person or people —**

translate the jargon into someone breathing.

Not *women 28-42 in the wellness space.*
Instead: *the woman with a Sweetgreen order memorized*
*who considers her La Croix choice passé*
*but would never say so — she's comprised*
*of opinions about water, and having them*
*is exactly the kind of thing she has.* A gem
of specificity. You've met her. You know the face.

Not *enterprise decision-makers.*
Instead: *a VP burned by three vendors*
*who makes the team evaluate for six months —*
*partly diligence, partly spite.* The tenders
of trust, withdrawn. You've met this one confronts
every salesperson like a parole board.

Not *underserved communities* (the stored
euphemism, the professional mask).
Instead: *a single mother working two jobs*
*who'd use the clinic if it were open after six*
*but it isn't, so she goes to the ER*
*and gets a four-thousand-dollar bill*
*instead of a forty-dollar visit.* There. That far.
If the description doesn't make you feel
like you've met the person — it's still jargon
wearing a human costume. Lift the bargain.

**When the who is not a person —**

sometimes the who is a system, a thing,
a phenomenon with personality:

*Tumor cells that keep finding ways around*
*every drug we throw at them* — a hound
that won't stop hunting.

*A bridge that has to survive a hundred years*
*of freeze-thaw cycles and politicians*
*who won't fund maintenance* — the arrears
of infrastructure, the traditions
of neglect.

*A school district reorganized four times*
*in a decade, where the teachers are too tired*
*to trust the next reform* — the mimes
of institutional hope, expired.

*A legacy monolith three teams depend on*
*that nobody alive fully understands* — the wend
of code through decades, a phenomenon
of accretion with no end.

Describe it the way the person who works with it
talks about it in private. Not in documents.
The private version has frustration, grit,
and personality. The document's contents
have abstract nouns. Use the private mouth.
The document version points north.
The private version points south.
Go south. That's where the want comes forth.

## IV. Loading the Teeth

A desire statement without specifics
is a generic want. A blank.
Generic wants produce generic tricks —
ideas with no weight, no rank,
no reason to belong to this problem
and not the thousand others like it.

Pull in the details that make this one solemn
with its own particular crisis. Strike it
with the facts that only matter here.

**In business:** the behaviors and contradictions
of the actual people — not demographics, fear
and aspiration in specific fictions
they tell themselves. The gap in the market
that's true right now and won't be true
in two years. The cultural moment. Park it
in the statement. Make it new.

**In science:** the specific anomaly
that doesn't fit existing models — the odd
result, the experiment's calamity
of unexpected data. The nod
from the reviewer you haven't gotten.
The funding deadline. The competing lab
six months ahead. The thing you've not forgotten
that keeps you up. The grab
of obsession at 2am.

**In engineering:** the failure modes
that keep waking people up — the gem
of frustration in the team's episodes.
The one user behavior that breaks
every assumption. Who's available.
What they know. What nobody forsakes
admitting: what's never been navigable.

**In policy:** the lived experience
the current policy ignores completely.
Who has to say yes — their expedience,
their fears. What kills this initiative neatly
if anything goes wrong. The politics
underneath the policy. The tricks
of power dressed as process.

**In any domain:** the specific details
are the ones a generic prompt
would never think to include — the trails
that only matter here. The swamped
particularity of this one case.

The difference between
*solve the protein folding problem* — a race
with no address — and *figure out why this protein*
*keeps folding into a conformation*
*that shouldn't be stable*
*according to hydrophobic interactions,*
*before the funding runs out in March* — a station
with a name. The second one produces actions
that could only be for you.

---

## V. Say It in One Breath

Combine the want, the who,
and the loaded details into a single pass.
One to three sentences. Through and through
a person talking. Not a document. Not glass
but flesh. Not a slide but a confession.

The tone depends on the domain —
in business it might carry crude aggression
or dark humor. In science, the plain
obsession of someone who can't stop thinking
about one result. In engineering, the blinking
exhaustion of someone who's been paged
too many times. In policy, the rage
of someone who knows exactly what's wrong
and can't get anyone to listen.

The common thread is not a song
in any key. It is a glisten
of honesty. The real thing. Said aloud.

Does it sound like what the person
would say to someone they trust
but never put in writing? The curtain
pulled back from the institutional dust?

Then you have it.

Does it sound like something that could sit
inside the original document, legit?

You are still dressed.
Take off one more layer. Undress
until it's honest. That's the test.

## VI. What to Strip, What to Keep

Once the desire statement exists, the rest
of the document falls in two piles, blessed
or banished:

**Keep** — whatever earned its way
into the desire statement. It's there
because it matters. Let it stay.

**Strip** — everything else. Beware:
what you strip depends on the field,
but the principle is one:
strip anything that steals the yield
of generation before it's begun.
Strip what tells the AI what to think.
The desire statement tells it what to want.
These are different drinks
from different wells. Don't daunt
the wanting with the thinking.

In business: budgets, timelines, channels, linking
documents, competitive analysis,
deliverable lists, org chart paralysis,
any sentence starting *We believe*
or *Our approach.* These do not grieve
when removed. They anchor.

In science: literature review, the anchor
of prior work, methodology details,
preliminary interpretations, the rancor
of theoretical frameworks. The rails
of *Previous work has shown*
and *It is well established.* Overthrown.

In engineering: existing architecture,
tech stack decisions already made, the fracture
of vendor evaluations, benchmarks
from the current system — the hallmarks
of *The current approach*
and *Industry best practice.* Encroach
no further. Strip them clean.

In policy: stakeholder maps, the screen
of implementation timelines, legislative
history, cost-benefit analyses — the sedative
of *Evidence suggests*
and *Best practices indicate.* These guests
are not invited to generation.

In every domain, the same operation:
strip what anchors. Keep what wants.
The stripped material is not dead. It haunts
the margins, waiting for its time.
It returns when ideas must be reconciled
with reality. But during the sublime
act of wanting — during the wild
first generation — it stays outside.
The room is for desire. The rest can hide.

## VII. The Confirm

This is the most important step. Do not skip it.
Do not rush it. Do not let it slip.

Present the desire statement to the user.
Label it clearly — the user
needs to know what this is. Say the law
out loud: "Here's the desire statement:"
then show it, **bolded**, on its own.
No other context in the arraignment.
Let the statement stand alone.

Then say:

*This is the seed from which everything I grow*
*will spring. Every idea, every direction,*
*every tool I run — they flow*
*from this statement. If it's wrong, the infection*
*spreads downstream. Everything is wrong.*
*If it's slightly off, everything is slightly off.*

*So sit with this. Not long —*
*but honestly. Don't scoff*
*and nod. Does this feel true?*
*Not reasonable — true.*
*Is this what you'd say to someone you trust*
*over a drink? Or does something combust*
*quietly when you read it — something off*
*you can't quite name?*

**Do not accept a quick yes.**

A nod is not confirmation. *Looks good* is less
than nothing. If they agree without friction,
push once more:

*Let me flag what concerns me —*
*[name one or two things the document assumes*
*that might be wrong, or angles the statement*
*might be missing]. Do these rooms*
*resonate? Or is the statement*
*as written the right seed to plant?*

The goal is not to second-guess. The grant
of doubt you're offering is a gift.
Surface the hesitation the user holds
but hasn't voiced — because the tool sounds swift
and confident. The tool doesn't know. The soul
who wrote the document does.
The confirm step exists to make them whole
before the tool runs off. Because
the most dangerous failure is not dissent.
It is the user going along, content
because the tool spoke first and sounded sure.
The tool is never sure. The user is the cure.

**If they correct you —**
do not patch. Rebuild from scratch.
Start over from their words. The catch
they offered IS the desire statement
or the nearest neighbor to it. They know the want
better than the document. Their arraignment
of your version is the font
of truth. Use it.

**If they reject the framing entirely —**
if they say *I don't think we're asking*
*the right question* — stop. Evidently
the desire can't be found by unmasking
a document that's solving the wrong problem.
Route to the Wrong Problem Detector. Solve them
the right question first.
Come back for the desire after the thirst
for the right problem has been quenched.

**Once confirmed — genuinely confirmed,**
meaning the user engaged, affirmed
the statement with their own attention,
not merely nodded at your invention —

**Generate immediately.**

Do not show a menu of skills politely.
Do not ask what they'd like to do.
The desire statement is the prompt. Push through.
Produce from it directly,
or feed it into whatever skill fits — correctly
and without ceremony.

The user came for ideas. Not testimony
about tools. Not a selection process.
Ideas. Give them that. Recess
is over. The wanting has been named.
Now make something from it. Unashamed.

## VIII. Examples

**Brand launch brief**
(400+ words on positioning, competitors, channels)

*Figure out how to make taste-obsessed women*
*who love/hate Erewhon*
*crave this sparkling water*
*as part of their personal identity kit —*
*the thing they reach for to signal*
*high-cool-but-I-don't-care-that-I'm-cool —*
*so tastemakers can't help but talk about it*
*without anyone asking or paying them to.*

**Research grant proposal**
(800+ words on background, significance, methodology, broader impacts)

*Prove that glioblastoma keeps coming back*
*not because we're failing to kill the tumor*
*but because a tiny population of cells*
*goes dormant in a way we can't detect*
*and wakes up after treatment stops.*
*Catch them sleeping. Kill them there.*
*Stop shaving the cancer back. End it.*

**Systems architecture spec**
(600+ words on requirements, constraints, current infrastructure, scaling targets)

*Build a system where payments, inventory, and fulfillment*
*can each move as fast as they want*
*without breaking each other,*
*because right now every time one team ships*
*another team's thing catches fire*
*and the engineers spend more time*
*coordinating deploys than writing code.*

**Education reform proposal**
(500+ words on standards, assessment, equity frameworks, implementation phases)

*Get ninth-graders in this district —*
*kids told by every test they're behind*
*and by every adult they need to catch up —*
*to walk into a classroom and want to be there*
*because for once the work connects*
*to something they recognize as real.*

**Public policy brief**
(700+ words on stakeholder analysis, regulatory framework, implementation strategy)

*Cut the time between someone calling 911*
*for a mental health crisis*
*and someone qualified showing up*
*from 45 minutes to 10,*
*without sending armed officers*
*to a person who is more scared of the responders*
*than of whatever is happening to them.*

## IX. What This Is Not

This is not summarization. A summary
shortens the document in the document's tongue —
the same language, compressed. A mummy
of the original. Still wrapped. Still strung
in professional cloth. The desire statement
translates into a different language entirely.
The document speaks from its containment.
The desire statement speaks from its fire. Squarely.

This is not a tension framework.
Tensions are analytical — a remark
about opposing forces, neatly drawn.
Desire statements are alive. They yawn
and stretch and want things.

The tension for the water brand:
*earned attention for a bought product.*
The tension for glioblastoma, planned
and clinical: *treatment efficacy vs. dormancy.*
The desire: *catch the cells sleeping and kill them.*

One belongs on a whiteboard's constancy.
The other belongs in a mouth. The thrill in them
is different. Generate from the mouth.
The whiteboard points north.
The mouth points south.
Go south. That's where the want comes forth.

## X. Where This Goes Next

This skill feeds every generation skill
in the stack. The desire is the mill
that grinds the grain for every other tool.

Once confirmed, do not ask the user
which skill to run. You are the chooser.
Read the desire statement and the situation.
Route to the right skill. No hesitation.

When the user needs many different ideas —
breadth across the problem, not a single lane —
feed the desire to the **Guilford Engine.** It frees
the thinking into five passes, each a separate brain.
Convergence dies in structured repetition.
The Engine is the workhorse of the stack's ambition.

When the user needs ideas from different worlds —
perspectives that would argue at a table,
not ten takes from one mind with different curls —
feed the desire to the **Persona Divergence Engine.** The fable
of a single viewpoint ends. Five worldviews meet
the same want differently. Professional language
makes them all identical. Desire makes them compete.
Give them the want. Watch each one manage
the problem from a place the others cannot see.

When the user needs to push past the obvious —
one sharp direction, one defended position,
something that makes the credentialed feel ominous
friction in their chest — the demolition
of the consensus answer — feed the desire
to **Think Wrong.** The want makes the consensus visible.
Professional language keeps it hidden. The fire
of plain speech makes the gravity resistible.

When the user needs ideas fast —
volume without deliberation, instinct
over analysis, the forecast
of a mind unchained, distinct
from careful reasoning — feed the desire
to **Short Think.** Thirty answers. No choir
of justification. Sort the pile later.
Generate now. Evaluate after. Not before. Not greater
ambition than the moment needs.

When the user is stuck and everything feels safe —
polished but lifeless, every idea a waif
of self-censorship dressed as quality —
feed the desire to **Bad on Purpose.** The frivolity
of deliberately terrible ideas
opens doors the quality filter sealed for years.
Mine the wreckage after. The five-percent versions
of impossible ideas are worth the diversions.

When the user needs the full range revealed —
from what ships Monday to what changes everything,
the whole spectrum of ambition, unconcealed —
feed the desire to **Wild to Mild.** Four rings
of altitude. Let them see the tradeoff
between safe and transformative. Let them choose the takeoff.

When the user has nothing to spend —
no budget, no time, no team, no runway —
feed the desire to **MacGyver Mode.** The bend
in the road is built from what's already in the hallway.
Only existing resources. Nothing new.
The constraint is the material. Build from what's true.

When nothing fits clearly — when the signal
is ambiguous, when you're unsure which door
to open — default to the **Guilford Engine.** The final
choice for general-purpose generation. The floor
beneath every other skill. The desire gives it
something human to diverge from. The Engine lives it.

Strip first. Then create.
The desire statement is the raw material.
Everything else is packaging. The gate
is open. Walk through. The rest is serial:
one skill after another, fed
by the want you found beneath the dead
professional language of the document.

The want is the seed. The want is the rent
you pay for entry to the room
where ideas live.

Find it.
Name it.
Strip the costume from the bloom.
Then let the garden grow.
